## Concepts

* [[AI in SOX Compliance]] — The application of artificial intelligence to SOX work — risk assessment, documentation, evidence gathering, testing, and fraud detection — with high perceived potential but low current adoption.
* [[Access Controls]] — Controls restricting who can access programs, data, and transactions — a core ITGC domain enforced through roles, permissions, provisioning workflows, and monitoring.
* [[Audit Trail]] — System-generated, tamper-evident logs of transactions, data changes, configuration changes, and logins — recording who did what and when — that provide forensic evidence for audits.
* [[Auditor Attestation Report]] — The external auditor's report under SOX 404(b) expressing a single opinion directly on the effectiveness of ICFR, filed with the annual report.
* [[Business Combinations]] — Acquisitions accounted for under ASC 805 — one of the most complex, judgment-intensive financial reporting areas, requiring tailored ICFR over valuation, journal entries, consolidation, and disclosures.
* [[Change Management]] — The ITGC discipline of controlling changes to programs, configurations, and roles — sandbox testing, documented approvals, and change logs — so systems keep operating as designed.
* [[Compensating Controls]] — Controls that mitigate the risk left by a deficient or missing control; called "mitigating controls" in SAP GRC terminology.
* [[Continuous Controls Monitoring]] — Automated, always-on monitoring of controls, transactions, configurations, and access — replacing periodic manual reviews with continuous detection of exceptions.
* [[Control Activities]] — Actions established through policies and procedures that help ensure management's risk-mitigation directives are carried out — the third COSO component.
* [[Control Environment]] — The set of standards, processes, and structures providing the basis for internal control across an organization — the first of the five COSO components, anchored by tone at the top.
* [[Controls Rationalization]] — Periodically reassessing and streamlining the controls portfolio to eliminate redundant controls, align controls to business risks, and reduce compliance cost.
* [[Disclosure Controls and Procedures]] — The controls evaluated quarterly under SOX Section 302 that ensure all required disclosure - not just financial statements - is recorded, processed, and reported timely.
* [[Emergency Access Management]] — Controlled, temporary elevated access ("firefighter" access) granted for emergencies under a formal regime of ownership, logging, and review.
* [[Enterprise Risk Management]] — The culture, capabilities, and practices organizations integrate with strategy-setting to manage risk in creating, preserving, and realizing value, as defined by COSO's 2017 ERM framework.
* [[Entity-Level Controls]] — Pervasive company-wide controls - control environment, override controls, monitoring, and period-end reporting controls - central to top-down ICFR scoping.
* [[Financial Reporting Risks]] — The risks that financial statements could be materially misstated, identified from knowledge of the business and GAAP as the starting point of the ICFR evaluation.
* [[Fraud Risk]] — The risk of material misstatement due to fraud, which both management's ICFR evaluation and the auditor's integrated audit must explicitly address.
* [[GRC Platform]] — A governance, risk, and compliance software system used to manage SOX scoping, controls documentation, testing, certifications, and deficiency tracking.
* [[ICFR]] — Internal control over financial reporting - the process providing reasonable assurance about the reliability of financial reporting and GAAP financial statement preparation.
* [[IT Application Controls]] — Automated controls embedded in applications — edit checks, automated matching, calculations, interfaces — that prevent or detect misstatements in transaction processing.
* [[IT General Controls]] — Controls over program development, program change, computer operations, and access to programs and data (ITGCs) that support the consistent operation of automated controls and key reports.
* [[Information Produced by the Entity]] — Reports and data generated by a company's own systems and used in performing or testing controls (commonly abbreviated IPE), whose completeness and accuracy must be validated.
* [[Information and Communication]] — The fourth COSO component — relevant, quality information and multidirectional communication supporting the functioning of internal control.
* [[Integrated Audit]] — A single engagement in which the audit of ICFR is integrated with the financial statement audit, with control tests serving both opinions.
* [[Internal Control]] — A process effected by an entity's board, management, and personnel to provide reasonable assurance of achieving operations, reporting, and compliance objectives.
* [[Journal Entry Controls]] — Controls over the preparation, approval, and posting of journal entries — a key antifraud and period-end reporting control area targeted at management override risk.
* [[Key Controls]] — Controls whose failure could materially affect objectives without timely detection by other controls, or whose operation prevents or detects other control failures.
* [[Least Privilege]] — The principle of granting users only the minimum access needed for their job duties, reducing fraud and error risk in financial systems.
* [[Management Assessment Process]] — Management's annual process under SOX 404(a) for evaluating and reporting on ICFR effectiveness, scalable by risk and supportable by daily interaction with controls.
* [[Management Override]] — The inherent ability of senior management to circumvent internal controls, a key inherent limitation of ICFR and a mandatory fraud-risk focus area.
* [[Management Review Controls]] — Controls in which management reviews estimates, analyses, or reports against expectations and thresholds — heavily relied on in judgmental areas and a persistent source of deficiencies and auditor scrutiny.
* [[Material Weakness]] — A deficiency, or combination of deficiencies, in ICFR such that there is a reasonable possibility that a material misstatement will not be prevented or detected on a timely basis.
* [[Monitoring of Internal Control]] — The fifth COSO component — ongoing evaluations, separate evaluations, or both, used to ascertain whether each component of internal control is present and functioning.
* [[Ongoing Monitoring]] — Monitoring procedures built into routine operations — supervisory activities, trend analysis, reconciliations, automated tools — that continuously evaluate internal control.
* [[Period-End Financial Reporting Process]] — The close process - journal entries, consolidating adjustments, and drafting statements and disclosures - which the auditor must always evaluate in an ICFR audit.
* [[Persuasive Information]] — Sufficient, suitable (relevant, reliable, timely) information — direct or indirect — that monitoring relies on to conclude whether internal control is operating effectively.
* [[Purchase Price Allocation]] — The allocation of an acquisition's consideration to acquired assets and assumed liabilities at fair value under ASC 805 (abbreviated PPA) — a critical, complex ICFR area.
* [[Reasonable Assurance]] — The level of assurance ICFR must provide - what would satisfy prudent officials in the conduct of their own affairs - explicitly not absolute assurance.
* [[Risk Appetite]] — The amount and type of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of value, established and aligned with strategy under the COSO ERM Framework.
* [[Risk Assessment]] — The dynamic, iterative process for identifying and analyzing risks to the achievement of objectives — the second COSO component and the foundation of risk-based SOX scoping.
* [[Robotic Process Automation]] — Software bots that automate repetitive, rules-based tasks — used both in business processes (requiring bot controls) and, increasingly, to execute SOX control procedures.
* [[Role-Based Access Control]] — Granting system access through roles that bundle permissions aligned to job duties (RBAC) — the standard ERP mechanism for enforcing least privilege and segregation of duties.
* [[SOC 1 Report]] — A service auditor's report on a service organization's controls relevant to user entities' internal control over financial reporting, used to monitor outsourced controls in SOX programs.
* [[SOX Compliance Program]] — The organizational program — governance, people, methodology, and technology — through which a public company sustains compliance with SOX Sections 302 and 404.
* [[SOX Modernization]] — Deloitte's call to refresh and rethink stale SOX programs through operating model optimization, program enhancements, and technology and automation.
* [[SOX Section 302]] — The Sarbanes-Oxley provision (15 U.S.C. 7241) requiring CEO/CFO certification of each annual and quarterly report and of internal control evaluation and disclosures.
* [[SOX Section 404]] — The Sarbanes-Oxley provision (15 U.S.C. 7262) requiring management's annual assessment of ICFR (404(a)) and the external auditor's attestation (404(b)).
* [[SOX Section 906]] — The Sarbanes-Oxley provision adding 18 U.S.C. 1350, a CEO/CFO certification of periodic reports backed by criminal penalties up to $5 million and 20 years.
* [[Safeguarding of Assets]] — The ICFR prong requiring reasonable assurance of prevention or timely detection of unauthorized acquisition, use, or disposition of assets that could materially affect the financial statements.
* [[Segregation of Duties]] — Dividing incompatible duties (e.g., creating vendors vs. paying them) among different people so no one person can both perpetrate and conceal errors or fraud.
* [[Separate Evaluations]] — Periodic, more objective assessments of internal control performed outside routine operations, used to reconfirm the effectiveness of ongoing monitoring.
* [[Significant Deficiency]] — An ICFR deficiency less severe than a material weakness yet important enough to merit attention by those responsible for oversight of financial reporting.
* [[Tone at the Top]] — The board's and senior management's expressed attitude toward integrity and internal control, which anchors the control environment and drives monitoring effectiveness.
* [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]] — The risk-based scoping approach - from financial statement level down through entity-level controls to significant accounts, assertions, and the controls to test.
* [[Total Cost of Control]] — The full cost of operating and testing a company's controls portfolio — a metric KPMG uses to benchmark and optimize ICFR programs.
* [[User Access Review]] — Periodic (quarterly/annual) review by managers of users' system access — also called access certification — verifying role assignments still match job duties.
* [[Walkthrough]] — Following a transaction from origination through the financial records using inquiry, observation, inspection, and re-performance to understand likely sources of misstatement.

## Frameworks

* [[COSO ERM Framework]] — COSO's Enterprise Risk Management — Integrating with Strategy and Performance (June 2017), organizing ERM into five components and twenty principles that link risk to strategy-setting and performance.
* [[COSO Internal Control Framework]] — COSO's Internal Control — Integrated Framework (1992, updated May 2013), the leading criteria for designing and assessing internal control, organized into three objective categories, five components, and seventeen principles.
* [[GAIT Methodology]] — The IIA's principles-based Guide to the Assessment of IT Risk, extended in March 2008 into a 10-step methodology for judging whether ITGC deficiencies rise to significant deficiencies or material weaknesses.
* [[ICFR Maturity Model]] — Consulting-firm maturity models (KPMG, PwC, Protiviti) that rate the health and sophistication of a company's ICFR/SOX program from basic compliance to value enhancement.
* [[Three Lines Model]] — The IIA's model (updated 2020) distributing risk and control responsibilities across management (first line), risk/compliance functions (second line), and internal audit (third line), used to structure SOX program operating models.

## Standards

* [[AS 2201]] — The PCAOB standard (originally Auditing Standard No. 5) governing an audit of ICFR integrated with an audit of financial statements under SOX 404(b).
* [[AU-C 940]] — The AICPA GAAS section (from SAS No. 130) governing an audit of ICFR integrated with a financial statement audit for non-issuers.
* [[GAAS]] — Generally Accepted Auditing Standards - the AICPA's clarified AU-C sections governing audits of non-issuers.

## Roles

* [[Audit Committee]] — The independent board committee that oversees accounting, financial reporting, and audits, hires the external auditor, and receives deficiency communications.
* [[External Auditor]] — The independent registered public accounting firm that audits the financial statements and attests to ICFR under SOX 404(b).
* [[Internal Audit]] — The third-line assurance function that tests controls, monitors management-override risk for the board, and whose work the external auditor may rely on.

## Organizations

* [[AICPA]] — The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, whose Auditing Standards Board issues GAAS (the AU-C sections) for audits of non-issuers, including AU-C 940 on integrated ICFR audits.
* [[AuditBoard]] — A vendor of purpose-built audit and controls management software (a GRC platform) that co-produced the 2024 SOX compliance survey with Protiviti.
* [[COSO]] — The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission, publisher of the internal control and ERM frameworks used for SOX 404 assessments.
* [[FEI]] — Financial Executives International, the association of ~15,000 CFOs and senior financial executives that advocates on SOX rulemaking and publishes preparer ICFR guidance.
* [[Grant Thornton]] — The accounting and advisory firm that authored COSO's 2009 Guidance on Monitoring Internal Control Systems under project leader Trent Gazzaway.
* [[IIA]] — The Institute of Internal Auditors, the internal audit profession's standards body, publisher of the GAIT guides and the Three Lines Model.
* [[PCAOB]] — The Public Company Accounting Oversight Board, created by SOX Title I to register, inspect, and discipline audit firms and to set auditing standards such as AS 2201.
* [[SEC]] — The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, the federal regulator that enforces the Sarbanes-Oxley Act and writes the rules governing management's ICFR reporting.

## Laws

* [[Foreign Corrupt Practices Act]] — The 1977 U.S. law whose books-and-records and internal accounting control provisions are the statutory ancestor of ICFR and the source of the "reasonable assurance" standard.
* [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]] — Public Law 107-204 (July 30, 2002), the U.S. federal statute that created the PCAOB, reformed auditor independence, and made CEOs and CFOs accountable for financial reports and internal control.
