The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (Public Law 107-204, H.R. 3763,
enacted July 30, 2002) is a U.S. federal law "to protect investors
by improving the accuracy and reliability of corporate
disclosures," passed in response to major accounting scandals
(notably Enron). The [[SEC]] enforces it.

Key facts:
- Eleven titles; Title I creates the [[PCAOB]] (Secs. 101-109);
  Title II restricts non-audit services and requires
  [[Audit Committee]] preapproval and audit partner rotation.
- [[SOX Section 302]] (Title III): CEO/CFO certification of each
  annual/quarterly report and of [[Internal Control]] evaluation.
- [[SOX Section 404]] (Title IV): management's annual [[ICFR]]
  assessment (404(a)) plus [[External Auditor]] attestation
  (404(b)); implemented by SEC Release 33-8238.
- [[SOX Section 906]] (Title IX): criminal CEO/CFO certification —
  up to $1M/10 years (knowing) or $5M/20 years (willful).
- Other provisions: Sec. 301 independent audit committees; Sec. 304
  clawback after restatements; Sec. 802 document-destruction
  penalties; Sec. 806 whistleblower protection.
