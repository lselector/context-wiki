Enterprise Risk Management (ERM), per the [[COSO ERM Framework]] (2017), is "the culture, capabilities, and practices that organizations integrate with strategy-setting and apply when they carry out that strategy, with a purpose of managing risk in creating, preserving, and realizing value." It addresses more than [[Internal Control]] — it also covers strategy-setting, governance, stakeholder communication, and performance measurement.

Key facts:

- Organized into five components (Governance and Culture; Strategy and Objective-Setting; Performance; Review and Revision; Information, Communication, and Reporting) and twenty principles.
- Emphasizes risk in **strategy selection** as well as risks to executing the chosen strategy, weighed against the entity's [[Risk Appetite]].
- Benefits: increased range of opportunities, entity-wide risk identification, reduced negative surprises and performance variability, improved resource deployment, enhanced resilience.
- In practice, corporate risk committees run annual enterprise risk assessments using the ERM framework to prioritize monitoring (COSO monitoring guidance example).
- [[KPMG]] notes the COSO ERM framework incorporates — but does not replace — the internal control framework for [[SOX Section 404]] purposes.
- Future trends: data proliferation and advanced analytics, AI and automation, and managing the cost of risk management by coordinating risk, compliance, control, and governance activities.
