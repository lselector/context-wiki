The Internal Control — Integrated Framework, issued by [[COSO]] in 1992 and updated in May 2013 (authored by [[PwC]] with [[SEC]] and [[PCAOB]] observers), is the framework most US public companies use for management's [[ICFR]] assessment under [[SOX Section 404]]. It defines [[Internal Control]] as a process, effected by the board, management, and other personnel, designed to provide [[Reasonable Assurance]] regarding objectives in operations, reporting, and compliance.

Key facts:

- Three objective categories: **operations** (including safeguarding of assets), **reporting** (internal/external, financial/non-financial), and **compliance** — depicted with the five components and entity structure as the "COSO cube."
- Five components: [[Control Environment]], [[Risk Assessment]], [[Control Activities]], [[Information and Communication]], and Monitoring Activities (see [[Monitoring of Internal Control]]).
- The 2013 update formalized **17 principles** across the five components (e.g., commitment to integrity, board oversight, fraud risk consideration, general controls over technology, ongoing and separate evaluations).
- Effectiveness requires each component and relevant principle to be **present and functioning**, and the five components to **operate together**; a major deficiency precludes effectiveness — the logic behind [[Material Weakness]] and [[Significant Deficiency]] evaluation.
- Limitations: faulty judgment, error, [[Management Override]], collusion, and external events — assurance is reasonable, not absolute.
- Complementary to (not superseded by) the [[COSO ERM Framework]]; COSO's monitoring guidance and an ICFR compendium extend it.
