## Overview

In May 2013, [[COSO]] (the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the
Treadway Commission) released the updated [[COSO Internal Control Framework]]
(Internal Control — Integrated Framework), superseding the widely used 1992
original. The Framework is the leading criteria for designing, implementing,
and assessing [[Internal Control]], and it is the framework most US public
companies use for management's [[ICFR]] assessment under [[SOX Section 404]]
of the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. The 2013 update retains the core definition
of internal control and the five components, while formalizing seventeen
principles, expanding reporting objectives beyond external financial
reporting, and reflecting changes in governance expectations, globalization,
technology, and fraud prevention. The framework was authored by PwC under
COSO commission, with [[SEC]] and [[PCAOB]] observers participating.

## Definition and objectives

Internal control is defined as "a process, effected by an entity's board of
directors, management, and other personnel, designed to provide reasonable
assurance regarding the achievement of objectives relating to operations,
reporting, and compliance." Key attributes: it is a process (a means to an
end), effected by people, provides [[Reasonable Assurance]] (not absolute),
and is adaptable to any entity structure.

Three categories of objectives:

- **Operations** — effectiveness and efficiency of operations, including
  performance goals and safeguarding of assets.
- **Reporting** — internal and external, financial and non-financial
  reporting (reliability, timeliness, transparency).
- **Compliance** — adherence to applicable laws and regulations.

The relationship between objectives (columns), the five components (rows),
and the entity's organizational structure (third dimension) is depicted as
the well-known COSO cube.

## The five components and seventeen principles

### 1. [[Control Environment]]

The set of standards, processes, and structures providing the basis for
internal control; the board and senior management set the
[[Tone at the Top]]. Principles:

1. Demonstrates commitment to integrity and ethical values.
2. The board demonstrates independence from management and exercises
   oversight of internal control (relevant to the [[Audit Committee]]).
3. Management establishes structures, reporting lines, authorities, and
   responsibilities.
4. Demonstrates commitment to attract, develop, and retain competent
   individuals.
5. Holds individuals accountable for internal control responsibilities.

### 2. [[Risk Assessment]]

A dynamic, iterative process for identifying and analyzing risks to the
achievement of objectives, forming the basis for how risks are managed.
Principles:

6. Specifies objectives with sufficient clarity to enable risk
   identification.
7. Identifies and analyzes risks across the entity.
8. Considers the potential for fraud in assessing risks.
9. Identifies and assesses changes that could significantly impact
   internal control.

### 3. [[Control Activities]]

Actions established through policies and procedures that ensure
management's risk-mitigation directives are carried out; preventive or
detective; manual or automated (authorizations, verifications,
reconciliations, business performance reviews). [[Segregation of Duties]]
is typically built in, with alternative controls where it is not practical.
Principles:

10. Selects and develops control activities that mitigate risks.
11. Selects and develops general control activities over technology
    (see [[IT General Controls]]).
12. Deploys control activities through policies and procedures.

### 4. [[Information and Communication]]

Relevant, quality information from internal and external sources supports
the functioning of internal control; communication flows up, down, and
across the entity and with external parties. Principles:

13. Obtains or generates and uses relevant, quality information.
14. Internally communicates internal control information and
    responsibilities.
15. Communicates with external parties regarding internal control matters.

### 5. Monitoring Activities

Ongoing evaluations, separate evaluations, or a combination ascertain
whether each component is present and functioning (see
[[Monitoring of Internal Control]]). Principles:

16. Selects, develops, and performs ongoing and/or separate evaluations.
17. Evaluates and communicates internal control deficiencies in a timely
    manner to those responsible for corrective action, including senior
    management and the board.

## Effective internal control and limitations

An effective system requires that (1) each of the five components and
relevant principles is **present and functioning**, and (2) the five
components **operate together** in an integrated manner. A "major
deficiency" in a component or principle precludes a conclusion of
effectiveness — the concept management maps to [[Material Weakness]] and
[[Significant Deficiency]] evaluations in the [[SOX Section 404]] and
[[SOX Section 302]] context. Limitations include faulty human judgment,
simple errors, [[Management Override]], collusion, and external events;
internal control provides reasonable, not absolute, assurance.

## Relationship to other COSO guidance

- The [[COSO ERM Framework]] and the Internal Control Framework are
  complementary; neither supersedes the other.
- COSO's earlier Guidance on Monitoring Internal Control Systems remains
  applicable to the updated Framework.
- A companion compendium, Internal Control over External Financial
  Reporting, illustrates applying the principles to [[ICFR]].
