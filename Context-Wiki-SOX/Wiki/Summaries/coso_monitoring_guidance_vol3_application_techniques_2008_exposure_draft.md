## Overview

This June 2008 exposure draft (public comment closed August 15, 2008) is
Volume III — Application Techniques of [[COSO]]'s Guidance on Monitoring
Internal Control Systems (see [[Monitoring of Internal Control]]).
Authored by Grant Thornton (project leader Trent Gazzaway) with a broad
task force including Big Four partners, GAO and [[SEC]] observers, it
illustrates, through dozens of short examples and three comprehensive case
studies, how organizations apply the monitoring principles of the
[[COSO Internal Control Framework]] in practice. The techniques are
explicitly examples, not "best practices" or requirements; users must be
familiar with the companion Guidance volume. Its structure parallels the
Guidance: establishing a foundation, designing and executing monitoring
procedures, and assessing and reporting results, plus comprehensive
examples and appendices. The final Volume III was published in 2009 and
is paywalled; this exposure draft documents the near-final content.

## The model for monitoring

Effective monitoring involves (1) establishing an effective foundation
for monitoring, (2) designing and executing monitoring procedures
prioritized based on risk, and (3) reporting the results and following up
on corrective action where necessary.

## Establishing a foundation for monitoring

The foundation comprises a [[Tone at the Top]] about the importance of
[[Internal Control]] (including monitoring), an organizational structure
covering the roles of management and the board and the use of evaluators
with appropriate capabilities and objectivity, and a baseline
understanding of internal control effectiveness. Examples show boards and
[[Audit Committee]]s setting expectations, and organizations assigning
monitoring roles across management, [[Internal Audit]], and line
personnel.

## Designing and executing monitoring procedures

The core is a logical design progression tied to risk:

- **Understand and prioritize risks.** Illustrations include a beverage
  manufacturer that focuses monitoring by location and risk priority
  (material or complex areas, significant system/process change, past
  errors, high turnover, prior self-assessment issues), with control-owner
  self-assessments rolled up through supervisory "Field Internal Control
  Assessment" reviews; an internet brokerage whose Corporate Risk
  Committee runs an annual enterprise risk assessment using the
  [[COSO ERM Framework]]; and a retail chain that groups risks (real
  estate, general accounting, loss prevention) into a risk matrix mapped
  to mitigating controls — practical forms of [[Risk Assessment]] and
  [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]] thinking.
- **Understand the internal control system and identify [[Key Controls]].**
  Key controls are those whose failure might materially affect objectives
  without timely detection, or whose operation prevents or detects other
  control failures. Examples show internal audit departments building
  audit programs around key controls, and rationale tables explaining why
  a given control (e.g., approval of sales above a limit, exception
  reports) is or is not selected as key for a specific risk.
- **Identify [[Persuasive Information]].** Direct versus indirect
  information, with examples of operating statistics, key risk
  indicators, and comparisons with expectations used as indirect
  evidence.
- **Implement monitoring procedures.** Combinations of
  [[Ongoing Monitoring]] built into operations and periodic
  [[Separate Evaluations]].

## Assessing and reporting results

Examples of prioritizing and communicating results, internal reporting
protocols (control owner plus at least one management level higher,
escalation to the board for severe items), and external reporting where
monitoring supports management's [[ICFR]] assertions under
[[SOX Section 404]] of the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]].

## Comprehensive examples

Chapter V walks three end-to-end cases through the full monitoring
process — prioritizing a risk, selecting key controls, identifying
persuasive information, executing monitoring procedures, and assessing
and reporting results:

1. **Large retail organization** — monitoring controls over store
   inventory.
2. **Mid-sized manufacturing organization** — monitoring controls over
   certain operational risks.
3. **Monitoring certain IT controls** — common IT-related financial
   reporting risks, the controls that mitigate them (including
   [[IT General Controls]]), IT management processes that can serve as
   monitoring activities, and technology tools used to monitor controls
   such as [[Segregation of Duties]] checks (an early articulation of
   [[Continuous Controls Monitoring]]).

Appendices provide supporting materials such as excerpts from an example
company risk matrix.
