## Overview

This [[KPMG]] Advisory guide (2004) answers frequently
asked questions about the [[Management Assessment Process]]
required by [[SOX Section 404]] of the
[[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. Written in the first compliance
wave under [[PCAOB]] Auditing Standard No. 2 (predecessor
of [[AS 2201]]), it targets management, SOX compliance
teams, and [[Audit Committee]] members, and organizes
guidance into four phases: planning/scoping, documenting
and evaluating design and operating effectiveness,
identifying and correcting deficiencies, and reporting on
internal controls. Note: management remains responsible
for compliance and much cited guidance ([[SEC]]/PCAOB
staff Q&As, AS No. 2 thresholds) has since evolved.

## Roles of management and the independent auditor

- Management must include an internal control report in
  the annual report stating its responsibility for
  [[Internal Control]] over financial reporting (ICOFR /
  [[ICFR]]) and its year-end effectiveness assessment.
- Management must accept responsibility for ICFR
  effectiveness, evaluate it against suitable criteria
  (such as the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]]),
  support the evaluation with sufficient evidence and
  documentation, and present a written assessment.
- The [[External Auditor]] attests to and reports on
  management's assessment; if management has not fulfilled
  its responsibilities, the auditor must disclaim an
  opinion.

## Section I — Planning and scoping the assessment

- Document ICFR at some level for all locations and
  business units; extent varies with financial
  significance. Acceptable documentation includes policy
  manuals, process models, flowcharts, job descriptions,
  memoranda, and self-assessment reports.
- Significant accounts: an account is significant if there
  is more than a remote likelihood of material
  misstatement; components of a caption (e.g., domestic
  vs. foreign receivables, allowance accounts) may be
  separately significant.
- Frameworks: [[COSO]] criteria are the norm; other
  suitable, recognized frameworks (CoCo, Turnbull Report)
  are permitted. The COSO Enterprise Risk Management
  framework incorporates — but does not replace — the
  1992 internal control framework.
- IT scope: applications and supporting infrastructure
  behind key processes, control objectives, and relevant
  assertions belong in scope, since IT supports
  initiation, authorization, recording, processing, and
  reporting of transactions ([[IT General Controls]] and
  [[IT Application Controls]], including feeder systems).

## Section II — Design and operating effectiveness

- Test the controls important to the assessment,
  generally: controls over transaction
  initiation/authorization/recording/reporting, accounting
  policy selection, antifraud programs, controls other
  controls depend on (including [[IT General Controls]]),
  controls over nonroutine/judgmental transactions, and
  company-level controls ([[Entity-Level Controls]]) such
  as tone at the top, the risk assessment process,
  monitoring, and the period-end financial reporting
  process.
- Prioritize controls ("key/primary" or high/medium/low)
  and pick the control point in the process flow that
  best addresses each risk — redundant controls need not
  all be tested.
- Testing [[Entity-Level Controls]] alone is not
  sufficient to conclude on ICFR effectiveness.
- Design effectiveness: test via inquiry plus observation
  or inspection, and most effectively by process
  walkthroughs; inquiry alone is not sufficient.
- Operating effectiveness: extent of testing depends on
  control nature, frequency, significance, and risk of
  failure (changes in volume, personnel, reliance on
  other controls, manual vs. automated, complexity).
  Illustrative minimum sample sizes: annual control 1;
  quarterly 2-3; monthly 2-4; weekly 5-10; daily 15-30;
  recurring manual controls 30-60. Automated controls may
  be tested with a single system query if design is
  validated.
- Management's testing, taken as a whole, should be more
  comprehensive than the auditor's.
- End-user computing (spreadsheets, flexible ERP
  reporting) needs proportionate general controls over
  access, change, development, and operations.
- Period-end financial reporting process controls
  (journal entries, consolidating adjustments, drafting
  statements and disclosures) are critical and must be
  tested for both annual and quarterly reporting.

## Section III — Identifying, assessing, and correcting deficiencies

- A [[Material Weakness]] can exist even if no material
  misstatement occurred — severity depends on the
  potential for misstatement.
- Remediation: a redesigned control needs enough time in
  operation (e.g., enough occurrences per management's
  sampling policy) to be tested; remediation after
  year-end ordinarily does not cure a deficiency for
  reporting purposes.
- Multiple [[Significant Deficiency]] items do not
  automatically aggregate to a [[Material Weakness]], but
  must be evaluated individually and in combination
  (same account, common assertion).
- AS No. 2-era definitions: a significant deficiency is a
  more-than-remote likelihood of a more-than-
  inconsequential misstatement; one quantitative guideline
  cited is potential misstatements of 1% or more of pretax
  earnings, always combined with qualitative judgment.
- Safeguarding of assets (per [[COSO]]) means preventing
  or detecting unauthorized acquisition, use, or
  disposition of assets that could materially affect the
  financial statements — about authorization, not business
  judgment quality.

## Section IV — Reporting on internal controls

Management includes its written assessment of ICFR
effectiveness as of fiscal year-end in the annual report;
identified [[Material Weakness]] items preclude an
"effective" conclusion and must be disclosed, and
significant deficiencies are communicated to the
[[Audit Committee]] and [[External Auditor]].
