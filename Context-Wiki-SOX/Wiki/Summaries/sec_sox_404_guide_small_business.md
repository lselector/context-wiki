## Overview

This official [[SEC]] staff brochure ("Sarbanes-Oxley
Section 404: A Guide for Small Business — it doesn't have
to be a chore") explains, in plain language, how smaller
public companies can comply with [[SOX Section 404]] of
the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. Section 404 requires annual
reports to include management's own assessment of
[[ICFR]] plus an [[External Auditor]] attestation; for
most smaller companies the management assessment first
applied to 2007 annual reports and the auditor attestation
was postponed to 2008. The brochure distills the SEC's
June 2007 interpretive guidance (Release 33-8810), which
was developed with smaller companies in mind and is
voluntary. (Source PDF is a scan; OCR quality is
mediocre.)

## Starting point

Maintaining [[Internal Control]] so that financial
statements are reliable is management's job in companies
of any size. The SEC has no prescriptive rules on how;
it points to the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]] —
including COSO's "Internal Control Over Financial
Reporting — Guidance for Smaller Public Companies" (2006)
and "Internal Control — Integrated Framework" (1992) —
as useful guidance from [[COSO]].

The certifying officers begin with two questions:

1. Do my employees understand what they need to do to
   properly prepare external financial reports?
2. What information do I need to make sure they have done
   those things?

## Step 1 — Identify financial reporting risks and controls

- Use knowledge of the business and of GAAP to identify
  where material misstatements could arise ("what could go
  wrong"): inherent internal/external risk factors, risks
  in authorizing/processing/recording transactions, and
  vulnerability to fraud — a scaled
  [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]].
- To match controls to risks, consider: how
  [[Entity-Level Controls]] relate to financial reporting
  elements and their precision; whether multiple controls
  address the same risk (pick the most efficient to
  evaluate); and whether a control is automated
  (how sturdy the relevant [[IT General Controls]] are)
  or manual (risk of human error).
- Only controls that adequately address financial
  reporting risks need to be identified — not every
  control in a process.
- In smaller companies with centralized reporting,
  management's daily involvement may itself provide the
  knowledge to identify risks and controls.

## Step 2 — Do your controls work in practice?

- Gather evidence of operating effectiveness in proportion
  to two risks: the risk of material misstatement in the
  financials and the risk that the control fails to operate
  as designed. Higher internal control risk means more
  evidence.
- Managers' own knowledge and supervision of controls —
  common in smaller companies — can limit the additional
  procedures needed.
- As risk increases, consider more objective personnel,
  more extensive validation, and testing over longer
  periods. Higher-risk elements include critical
  accounting policies; higher-risk controls include those
  subject to management override, involving significant
  judgment, or complex.
- A control is effective if it operates as designed, is
  properly applied, operates consistently, and is run by
  personnel with the authority and competence to do the
  job. Otherwise a control deficiency exists.

## Step 3 — Report conclusions and deficiencies

- The annual report includes management's assessment of
  overall ICFR effectiveness. If any deficiency — alone or
  in combination — is a [[Material Weakness]], management
  cannot conclude controls are effective.
- A [[Material Weakness]] is one or more control
  deficiencies creating a reasonable possibility of a
  material misstatement in annual or interim financial
  statements — a misstatement need not have occurred.
  Factors: susceptibility of the element to loss or fraud
  and the size of exposed amounts.
- Material weaknesses must be described in the annual
  report, ideally with an analysis of their impact and
  remediation plans, and communicated to the
  [[Audit Committee]] and [[External Auditor]] along with
  lesser-but-important deficiencies, defined as a
  [[Significant Deficiency]] in SEC rules.

## Records and further help

- Management must keep reasonable support for its
  assessment: written records of control design, how
  evidence was gathered and evaluated, and the basis for
  the effectiveness conclusion; existing books and records
  may suffice for a smaller company.
- Pointers to further guidance: the SEC's June 2003
  implementing rules (33-8238), June 2007 interpretive
  guidance (33-8810), rules defining material weakness
  (33-8809) and significant deficiency (33-8829), staff
  FAQs, and the Office of the Chief Accountant's
  404smallbusiness@sec.gov help channel.
