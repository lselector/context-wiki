## Overview

Issued in January 2009 under COSO chair Larry Rittenberg, with Grant
Thornton as author (project leader Trent Gazzaway), this guidance
elaborates on the monitoring component of the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]] — see [[Monitoring of Internal Control]]. [[COSO]] observed
that many organizations underutilized monitoring: they either failed to
leverage existing monitoring to support conclusions about [[ICFR]]
effectiveness (adding redundant year-end procedures instead), or lacked
effective ongoing monitoring altogether. The guidance helps organizations
"build in" rather than "add on" monitoring, improving both effectiveness
and efficiency — a direct response to the cost pressures of
[[SOX Section 404]] compliance under the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. The raw
PDF (236 pp.) bundles all three volumes: Volume I Guidance, Volume II
Application (with Appendix A reproducing the 20 principles of COSO's 2006
guidance for smaller public companies and a glossary), and Volume III
Examples.

## Nature and purpose of monitoring

Monitoring "ensures that internal control continues to operate
effectively" and provides value in three ways:

- Enables management and the board to determine that all five components
  of [[Internal Control]] continue to operate effectively over time,
  supporting external assertions such as [[SOX Section 302]] and
  [[SOX Section 404]] certifications.
- Provides timely evidence of changes so deficiencies are corrected
  before they materially affect objectives.
- Promotes good control operation — people perform duties properly when
  they know their work is subject to oversight.

Internal control systems fail because they were badly designed at the
outset, because the environment changed (risks, people, processes,
technology) without corresponding design changes, or because their
operation changed. Ineffective monitoring allows natural deterioration of
control systems over time.

## The model for monitoring

The guidance articulates a three-stage monitoring model.

### 1. Establish a foundation for monitoring

- **[[Tone at the Top]]** — management's and the board's expressed
  beliefs about the importance of monitoring drive its effectiveness.
- **Organizational structure** — management owns the internal control
  system; the board (including the [[Audit Committee]]) provides
  governance and oversight, and should arrange objective monitoring of
  controls senior management cannot self-monitor, such as the risk of
  [[Management Override]] — often via the [[Internal Audit]] function.
- **Evaluators** — monitoring is conducted by evaluators with adequate
  **competence** (knowledge of controls and what constitutes a
  deficiency) and **objectivity** (no vested interest in the results).
- **Baseline** — a supported baseline understanding of the internal
  control system's design and operation is the starting point; the cycle
  is control baseline, change identification, change management, and
  control revalidation/update.

### 2. Design and execute monitoring procedures

A four-step logical progression:

1. **Prioritize risks** — link monitoring to the [[Risk Assessment]]
   component; focus on "meaningful risks" that could have a consequential
   effect on objectives.
2. **Identify [[Key Controls]]** — controls whose failure could
   materially affect objectives without timely detection by other
   controls, or whose operation prevents/detects other control failures;
   key controls may reside in any of the five components, including the
   [[Control Environment]] and [[Control Activities]].
3. **Identify [[Persuasive Information]]** — sufficient, suitable
   (relevant, reliable, timely) information. **Direct information**
   (observation, reperformance) gives an unobstructed view of control
   operation; **indirect information** (operating statistics, key risk
   and performance indicators, comparative industry metrics) can flag
   anomalies but loses persuasive power over time and must periodically
   be revalidated with direct information.
4. **Implement monitoring** — through [[Ongoing Monitoring]] (built into
   routine operations: supervisory activities, trend analysis,
   reconciliations, automated tools) and/or [[Separate Evaluations]]
   (periodic, more objective assessments that reconfirm ongoing
   monitoring). Higher risk shortens the interval between separate
   evaluations.

### 3. Assess and report results

- Prioritize identified deficiencies by likelihood of material effect,
  effectiveness of [[Compensating Controls]], and aggregation of
  multiple deficiencies — the same logic used in [[Material Weakness]]
  and [[Significant Deficiency]] evaluation.
- Report each deficiency to the person responsible for the control and
  to management at least one level higher; severe deficiencies go to the
  board; suspected fraud bypasses the responsible person and goes to
  senior management and the board.
- Externally, well-designed monitoring supports assertions and can be
  tuned (more objective evaluators, more direct information, more formal
  documentation) so the [[External Auditor]] and regulators can rely on
  it, improving audit efficiency under [[AS 2201]].

## Other considerations

- **Outsourced controls** — user organizations must understand and
  monitor risks at service providers, typically via independent audit or
  examination reports (service auditor reports) or their own separate
  evaluations.
- **Technology** — automated control monitoring tools ("continuous
  controls monitoring," e.g., threshold checks, duplicate-payment
  detection, [[Segregation of Duties]] analysis) and workflow-based
  process management tools enhance monitoring; their own reliability
  depends on [[IT General Controls]].
- **Documentation and scalability** — smaller organizations with direct
  senior-management knowledge need less formal documentation; external
  reporting requirements push all organizations toward more formality.
  Monitoring scales with organizational size and complexity and is not
  one-size-fits-all.

## Summary principles

Ten summary considerations include: follow a systematic process for what
and how to monitor; monitor how the whole system addresses meaningful
risks, not isolated control activities; the board oversees monitoring of
tone at the top and management-override risk; use ongoing monitoring over
separate evaluations where feasible; indirect information can be relied
on only for a finite period without direct support; and evaluate,
report, and correct identified deficiencies. The guidance closes with
effectiveness and efficiency self-assessment questions for management
and boards.
