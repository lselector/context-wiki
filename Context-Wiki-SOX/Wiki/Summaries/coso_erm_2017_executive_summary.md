## Overview

In June 2017, [[COSO]] published Enterprise Risk Management — Integrating
with Strategy and Performance, updating its 2004 Enterprise Risk
Management — Integrated Framework. The updated [[COSO ERM Framework]]
positions [[Enterprise Risk Management]] not as a function, department, or
checklist, but as "the culture, capabilities, and practices that
organizations integrate with strategy-setting and apply when they carry out
that strategy, with a purpose of managing risk in creating, preserving, and
realizing value." It was authored by PwC for the COSO Board. The framework
is complementary to the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]]; the two are
distinct, neither supersedes the other, and ERM addresses more than
[[Internal Control]] — it also covers strategy-setting, governance,
stakeholder communication, and performance measurement.

## Why the update

Since 2004, risk complexity has grown, new risks have emerged, and boards
have enhanced their risk oversight while demanding better risk reporting.
The update:

- Connects ERM to stakeholder expectations and positions risk in the
  context of performance rather than as an isolated exercise.
- Emphasizes risk in both **strategy selection** and **driving
  performance**.
- Accommodates governance expectations, globalization, greater business
  complexity, evolving technology, and data/analytics proliferation.

## Role of risk in strategy selection

Beyond the traditional focus on "risks to the existing strategy," the
framework highlights two aspects with potentially greater effect on value:

1. **The possibility of strategy not aligning** with the organization's
   mission, vision, and core values.
2. **The implications from the strategy chosen** — each alternative
   strategy carries its own risk profile, which the board and management
   must weigh against the entity's [[Risk Appetite]].

## The five components

The Framework is a set of principles organized into five interrelated
components spanning mission through enhanced value:

1. **Governance and Culture** — governance sets the organization's tone
   and oversight responsibilities for ERM; culture pertains to ethical
   values, desired behaviors, and understanding of risk (parallels the
   [[Control Environment]] and [[Tone at the Top]]).
2. **Strategy and Objective-Setting** — ERM, strategy, and
   objective-setting work together; a risk appetite is established and
   aligned with strategy; business objectives put strategy into practice.
3. **Performance** — risks to strategy and business objectives are
   identified, assessed by severity, and prioritized in the context of
   risk appetite; risk responses are selected and a portfolio view of
   risk is developed (extends the [[Risk Assessment]] discipline).
4. **Review and Revision** — reviewing entity performance to consider
   how well ERM components function over time and in light of substantial
   change (analogous to [[Monitoring of Internal Control]]).
5. **Information, Communication, and Reporting** — a continual process of
   obtaining and sharing information from internal and external sources,
   flowing up, down, and across the organization (parallels
   [[Information and Communication]]).

## The twenty principles

- **Governance and Culture:** 1. Exercises board risk oversight;
  2. Establishes operating structures; 3. Defines desired culture;
  4. Demonstrates commitment to core values; 5. Attracts, develops, and
  retains capable individuals.
- **Strategy and Objective-Setting:** 6. Analyzes business context;
  7. Defines risk appetite; 8. Evaluates alternative strategies;
  9. Formulates business objectives.
- **Performance:** 10. Identifies risk; 11. Assesses severity of risk;
  12. Prioritizes risks; 13. Implements risk responses; 14. Develops
  portfolio view.
- **Review and Revision:** 15. Assesses substantial change; 16. Reviews
  risk and performance; 17. Pursues improvement in ERM.
- **Information, Communication, and Reporting:** 18. Leverages
  information and technology; 19. Communicates risk information;
  20. Reports on risk, culture, and performance.

## Benefits and board oversight

Benefits of effective ERM include increasing the range of opportunities,
identifying and managing risk entity-wide, reducing negative surprises and
performance variability, improving resource deployment, and enhancing
enterprise resilience. The board's risk oversight role includes reviewing
and challenging proposed strategy and risk appetite, significant business
decisions, responses to performance fluctuations and deviations from core
values, and approving management incentives — responsibilities that in
public companies intersect with [[Audit Committee]] duties and the
governance expectations underlying the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]].

## Looking into the future

Trends expected to shape ERM: proliferation of data and advanced
analytics, artificial intelligence and automation, managing the cost of
risk management by coordinating risk/compliance/control/governance
activities, and building stronger, more resilient organizations.
