## Overview

[[SEC]] Interpretive Release Nos. 33-8810 / 34-55929
(Federal Register, June 27, 2007) provides voluntary
guidance for management's evaluation and assessment of
[[ICFR]] under [[SOX Section 404]] of the
[[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. An evaluation that complies with
this guidance is one way to satisfy Exchange Act Rules
13a-15(c) and 15d-15(c). The release responds to feedback
that early Section 404 compliance was too costly, and it
gives management — especially smaller companies —
flexibility to scale the [[Management Assessment Process]]
using a [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]] approach. Companion
rules define [[Material Weakness]] and amend the
[[External Auditor]] attestation requirements.

## Two organizing principles

1. Management should evaluate whether it has implemented
   controls that adequately address the risk that a
   material misstatement of the financial statements
   would not be prevented or detected in a timely manner
   — a top-down, risk-based approach in which
   [[Entity-Level Controls]] play a central role.
2. Management's evaluation of evidence about the
   operation of its controls should be based on its
   assessment of risk — more efficient methods such as
   self-assessment in low-risk areas, more extensive
   testing in high-risk areas.

Management may use a different testing approach than the
auditor, is not required to identify every control in a
process, and need only document controls that adequately
address material-misstatement risk.

## Key concepts and definitions

- [[ICFR]]: a process designed under supervision of the
  principal executive and financial officers to provide
  reasonable assurance regarding the reliability of
  financial reporting and preparation of GAAP financial
  statements, covering record maintenance, transaction
  authorization, and safeguarding of assets.
- Reasonable assurance: keyed to FCPA language — the
  level "as would satisfy prudent officials in the conduct
  of their own affairs"; not absolute assurance, since
  ICFR can be defeated by human error, collusion, or
  management override.
- [[Material Weakness]]: a deficiency, or combination of
  deficiencies, in ICFR such that there is a reasonable
  possibility that a material misstatement of annual or
  interim financial statements will not be prevented or
  detected on a timely basis. If one exists, management
  may not conclude ICFR is effective.
- [[Significant Deficiency]]: lesser deficiencies that
  must be reported to the [[Audit Committee]] and the
  [[External Auditor]] (definition was under further SEC
  comment at the time).
- [[Entity-Level Controls]]: pervasive controls such as
  the control environment (tone, integrity, board/audit
  committee oversight), controls over management override,
  the risk assessment process, centralized/shared-service
  processing, monitoring controls (internal audit,
  self-assessment), and period-end financial reporting
  controls.
- Suitable control framework: the assessment must be made
  against a recognized framework such as the
  [[COSO Internal Control Framework]] (also CoCo and the
  Turnbull Report are cited as suitable).

## The evaluation process

### 1. Identifying financial reporting risks and controls

- Start by identifying and assessing risks to reliable
  financial reporting (materially accurate statements),
  including changes in those risks, using knowledge of the
  business and GAAP.
- Evaluate whether controls placed in operation are
  designed to adequately address those risks;
  [[Entity-Level Controls]] can themselves adequately
  address a risk, eliminating the need to evaluate other
  controls for that risk.
- Role of [[IT General Controls]]: controls may be
  automated ([[IT Application Controls]]), IT-dependent,
  or manual. Management evaluates only those ITGCs
  (program development, program change, computer
  operations, access to programs and data) necessary for
  the proper and consistent operation of controls that
  address financial reporting risks — not ITGCs relevant
  only to operational efficiency. IT evaluation is an
  integral part of the top-down approach, not a separate
  exercise.
- Evidential matter: management must maintain reasonable
  support (documentation) for its assessment; form and
  extent vary with size, nature, and complexity.

### 2. Evaluating evidence of operating effectiveness

- The evidence needed depends on ICFR risk: the risk of
  material misstatement in a financial reporting element
  and the risk that a control might fail to operate.
- Daily interaction, self-assessment, and ongoing
  monitoring can serve as evidence, particularly in
  low-risk areas and smaller companies.

### 3. Multiple locations

Scope and evidence at individual locations follow the
same risk-based logic, considering centralization and
commonality of controls.

## Reporting considerations

- Evaluate each control deficiency, individually and in
  combination (quantitative and qualitative factors,
  magnitude of potential misstatement, likelihood of
  failure); deficiencies affecting the same amount or
  disclosure may aggregate to a [[Material Weakness]].
- Severity depends on the possibility of misstatement,
  not on whether a misstatement actually occurred.
- Management must state whether ICFR is effective;
  a [[Material Weakness]] must be disclosed in the annual
  report, ideally with its nature, impact on financial
  reporting, and remediation plans, distinguishing
  pervasive from limited weaknesses.
- [[Significant Deficiency]] items go to the
  [[Audit Committee]] and auditors under the
  [[SOX Section 302]] certification rules (13a-14).
- The release also addresses restatement impact on prior
  ICFR reports and inability to assess certain aspects.
