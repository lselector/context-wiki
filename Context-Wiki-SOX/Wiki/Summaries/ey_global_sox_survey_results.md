## Overview

This [[EY]] global SOX survey (published 2020) examines how
organizations manage and enhance their
[[SOX Compliance Program]], the challenges they face, and
how technology is used globally. Respondents were publicly
traded companies of all sizes (34% with revenue over $10b,
47% between $1b and $10b, 19% under $1b); 80% had complied
with the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]] or similar legislation for
more than five years. EY notes SOX-like internal control
laws have spread to Japan, Canada, South Africa, and Korea,
with the UK and Netherlands considering similar regimes.

## Headline findings

- More than **87%** of respondents see their main objective
  as maintaining a lean, effective [[Internal Control]]
  framework, with significant [[External Auditor]] reliance
  on management's work.
- More than **37%** leverage SOX to enhance management
  skills, experience, and accountability over controls.
- Despite 20 years of technology advances, **63%** say the
  level of effort to execute SOX has *increased* since
  initial implementation, and 16% say it stayed the same.
- Top challenges to the control environment: rapid changes
  in systems and technologies; number and complexity of IT
  environments (legacy systems); increasing demands from
  external auditors; SOX being viewed as a compliance
  exercise rather than a value-added program.

EY organizes its recommendations around three themes:
strong connected governance and oversight; a responsive,
risk-based operating model enabled by technology; and
continuous program improvement.

## Theme 1: Strong, connected governance and oversight

- Only **36%** of SOX programs have a formal steering
  committee composed of all key stakeholders; EY recommends
  expanding it beyond controllership, IT, and
  [[Internal Audit]].
- Business events drive deficiencies: in the past three
  years respondents saw newly adopted accounting standards
  (87%), M&A (67%), new ERP implementations (37%, of which
  37% caused increased control deficiencies), divestitures,
  external auditor rotation (33%), and significant business
  transformation (33%).
- IT complexity is a key governance challenge: 72% run a
  combination of in-house and off-the-shelf systems, 56%
  use large ERPs, and ERP customization rates are high
  (JD Edwards 92%, Oracle 82%, SAP 80%).
- **[[IT General Controls]] produced the most open
  deficiencies at the end of the SOX cycle**, giving rise
  to more instances of a [[Significant Deficiency]] and
  [[Material Weakness]] than any other aspect of SOX, and
  were the most time-consuming to remediate.
- Benefits reported from SOX compliance: improved internal
  control environment (42%), better risk control (28%),
  streamlined control activities (25%).
- **49%** reassess/rationalize controls annually; reasons:
  increase efficiencies/synergies (86%), align SOX with
  business risks (72%), reduce costs (59%).
- External auditor reliance on management's testing: 43%
  for IT, 40% for business process; where little reliance
  exists, 50% are unsure why and 22% cite methodology
  standards not agreed with the auditor. EY recommends a
  program playbook, shared testing templates, and aligned
  documentation to increase reliance.

## Theme 2: Responsive, risk-based operating model enabled by technology

- **91%** use quantitative and qualitative data for
  scoping, but **46%** scope only once at the start of the
  year; EY recommends refreshing scoping at least
  semiannually — a dynamic [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]].
- Emerging areas covered by SOX controls: accounting
  estimates (95%), adoption of new accounting standards
  (91%), third-party service providers (87%), related
  parties (83%), business combinations (75%), cybersecurity
  disclosures (46%), budgetary process (46%), non-GAAP
  measures (41%).
- **79%** assess third-party service organizations via a
  [[SOC 1 Report]].
- Testing coverage: 41% test through month 12; 77% perform
  inquiries to confirm no significant changes before
  year-end.
- Most challenging areas for reliable, sustainable
  controls: IT general/application controls (64%) — both
  [[IT General Controls]] and [[IT Application Controls]] —
  information produced by the entity / [[Information Produced by the Entity|IPE]] (49%), and
  [[Management Review Controls]] (41%).
- Three out of four companies see emerging technology
  adoption as the leading SOX transformation lever for the
  next three years (77%), followed by upskilling personnel
  (54%); funding is the biggest barrier.
- Data analytics is the most prevalent technology (used
  often by ~1 in 5); [[Continuous Controls Monitoring]] is
  used often by 17%; [[Robotic Process Automation]] is used
  by fewer than 16% of SOX programs, and where the business
  uses RPA, 66% of programs test the bots.
- Top analytics challenges: data quality (62%), training
  (54%), appropriate tools (48%), resources (48%).

## Theme 3: Continuous program improvement

- 54% agree upskilling or adding technical resources is key
  to transforming SOX in the next three years.
- 39% train process/control owners annually; 35% train when
  requirements materially change (e.g.,
  [[COSO Internal Control Framework]] 2013).
- On average ~30% of in-scope key controls are classified
  as manual; control mix also includes ITGCs (18%),
  IT-dependent manual (14%), [[Information Produced by the Entity|IPE]] (10%),
  [[Management Review Controls]] (8%), fully automated
  application controls (8%), [[Entity-Level Controls]]
  (6%), and key spreadsheets (5%). EY challenges whether
  "manual" classifications are still accurate.
- Only **46%** perform true end-to-end walkthroughs of a
  single transaction annually; 83% determine in-scope IT
  systems from business processes — a possible cause of
  persistent ITGC deficiencies.
- 57% document processes with combined flowcharts and
  narratives; 82% have designed thresholds for review
  controls; of material weaknesses reported, **30%** were
  attributed to inadequate manual review controls or
  deficiencies in existing review controls.

## Recommendations (call to action)

- Evolve the operating model with a flexible, dynamic
  approach to managing internal controls.
- Innovate through technology: digital risk assessments,
  automated scoping tools, analytic testing procedures.
- Upskill the workforce; cross-train on business processes
  and [[IT General Controls]].
- Build trust with the business through feedback loops.
- Challenge the nature, timing, and extent of testing and
  the accuracy of manual vs. automated classifications.
- Hold a facilitated visioning session on governance,
  operating model, talent, technology, and strategy.
