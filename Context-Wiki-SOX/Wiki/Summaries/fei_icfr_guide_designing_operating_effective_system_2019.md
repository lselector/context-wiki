## Overview

This December 2019 guide from [[FEI]]'s Committee on Corporate Reporting
(CCR) — part of its "ICFR: Insights, Issues and Practices" series — helps
preparers design and operate an effective system of [[ICFR]] over
**business combinations** (acquisitions), one of the most complex,
judgment-intensive areas of financial reporting. It targets compliance
with Section 404(a) of the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]] (management's
[[SOX Section 404]] assessment) and reflects the persistent preparer
challenge of translating high-level [[Internal Control]] theory from
principles (such as the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]]) into
practice. The document is explicitly non-authoritative: its example risks
and [[Control Activities]] must be tailored to each company's own
[[Risk Assessment]], materiality, and transaction-specific facts and
circumstances.

## Approach

The first step is identifying all risks that present a reasonable
possibility of material misstatement related to the acquisition; the
nature and level of risk varies acquisition by acquisition. Controls are
then tailored to those risks — including precision levels, expectations
versus actuals, and follow-up on outliers. Bracketed financial statement
assertions (completeness, existence, valuation, accuracy) accompany each
example risk. Three organizing questions:

1. How can companies design and operate internal controls related to
   the identification and valuation of assets acquired and liabilities
   assumed?
2. How can companies design and operate internal controls for other
   acquisition accounting concepts — manual journal entries,
   consolidations, disclosures, and measurement period adjustments?
3. How can companies design and operate internal controls around
   accounting policy alignment?

## Management review controls

Business combinations rely heavily on [[Management Review Controls]] due
to complexity, subjectivity, and estimation uncertainty. Design and
operation questions include: the control's objective and risks addressed;
the control owner's competence and authority; procedures validating
completeness and accuracy of data used in the control (see
[[Information Produced by the Entity]]); what is reviewed (significant
assumptions, data, methodology); quantitative/qualitative thresholds
calibrated to detect a [[Material Weakness]]-level misstatement; and
follow-up procedures for items exceeding thresholds. Recommended evidence
practices: keep a list of follow-up items and resolutions, retain
multiple iterations of calculations showing adjustments, keep email
threads of reviewer questions and preparer responses, and document formal
review meetings in minutes — evidence the [[External Auditor]] will need
when testing the controls under [[AS 2201]].

## Valuation of assets acquired and liabilities assumed

Under ASC 805, acquired assets and assumed liabilities are measured at
acquisition-date fair value; the [[Purchase Price Allocation]] (PPA) is
one of the most critical and complex steps. Example risk/control pairs
have a "relevant competent leader" (who may sit outside accounting —
e.g., FP&A, tax, or M&A):

- Review target data (historical accounting records, audited financial
  statements) and ASC 805 guidance to ensure all acquired assets and
  assumed liabilities, including intangibles apart from goodwill, are
  identified [completeness, existence].
- Review the appropriateness of the valuation approach (income, market,
  cost) and method (MPEEM, relief from royalty, distributor method)
  for each significant asset or liability [valuation].
- Review the specialist's valuation end-to-end, including prospective
  financial information (PFI), data, assumptions, and methods, with
  involvement throughout the external specialist's engagement
  [accuracy, completeness].
- Review identification and calculation of significant PFI components
  (revenue, opex, EBITDA, capex, depreciation, net working capital).
- Review sensitivity analyses on significant assumptions (growth rates,
  customer attrition, royalty rates, contributory asset charges, tax
  rates, discount rates), consider alternatives and contrary evidence,
  and compare against historical/industry data and other company PFI.

Risk generally increases with the transaction's significance; the share
of goodwill relative to consideration transferred (lower for mature
targets, higher for growth targets) is a useful reasonableness check.

## Other acquisition accounting and policy alignment

Additional sections cover controls over manual journal entries (see
[[Journal Entry Controls]]) recorded for the acquisition, consolidation
of the target, financial statement disclosures, measurement period
adjustments, and aligning the target's accounting policies with the
acquirer's — plus integrating the target into the acquirer's
[[Control Environment]]. Appendix A gives account-level risk
considerations; Appendix B summarizes the example risk and control
activities.
