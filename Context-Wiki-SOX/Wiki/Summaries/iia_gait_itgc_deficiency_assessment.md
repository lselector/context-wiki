## Overview

This 25-page practice guide from the [[IIA]] (The Institute of
Internal Auditors, March 2008) extends the [[GAIT Methodology]]
to the assessment of [[IT General Controls]] (ITGC)
deficiencies identified during [[SOX Section 404]] assessments
of [[ICFR]]. It updates the 2004 "Framework for Evaluating
Control Exceptions and Deficiencies" for the post-AS 5 world
(the [[PCAOB]] standard that replaced AS 2) and aligns with the
top-down, risk-based scoping approach recommended by the
[[SEC]] and [[PCAOB]]. The guide is intended for management,
internal auditors, and the [[External Auditor]] alike when
deciding whether an ITGC failure is a mere control deficiency,
a [[Significant Deficiency]], or a [[Material Weakness]].

## The reliance chain

The core insight is that ITGC deficiencies do not directly
misstate the financial statements. Their effect travels through
a "reliance chain," which is the [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]]
scoping logic traveled in reverse:

- Individual key ITGCs support ITGC control objectives.
- ITGC control objectives protect the consistent operation of
  critical IT functionality — automated [[IT Application Controls]], key reports, calculations, and updates — inside
  financially significant applications.
- That critical functionality, together with manual and
  [[Entity-Level Controls]], prevents or detects material
  misstatements in significant accounts.

An ITGC failure therefore only matters to the extent it
creates at least a reasonable possibility that a key automated
control or key report fails undetected, leading to a material
misstatement. The burden of proof is placed on demonstrating
why a key control failure is NOT a [[Material Weakness]].

## Six assessment principles

1. Assessing ITGC deficiencies requires understanding the
   reliance chain between the financial statements and the
   failed ITGC key controls.
2. A [[Material Weakness]] requires meeting two tests:
   likelihood (at least a reasonable possibility of error) and
   impact (the potential error would be material).
3. Because the effect is indirect, the assessment proceeds in
   stages, with likelihood and impact applied across the
   combination of steps.
4. All ITGC deficiencies relating to the same ITGC control
   objective are assessed as a group.
5. All failed ITGC control objectives relating to the same key
   automated controls, key reports, or other critical
   functionality are assessed as a group.
6. Aggregation: control deficiencies of all types (manual and
   automated) relating to the same significant account or
   disclosure are considered together.

## The 10-step assessment process

1. Confirm testing exceptions represent a control failure, not
   an isolated exception.
2. Identify the ITGC control objective(s) the control supports.
3. Determine whether the ITGC control objective(s) were
   achieved (considering compensating controls within ITGC).
4. Identify which financially significant applications rely on
   those control objectives.
5. Decide whether normal operations would likely detect the
   failure — if yes, classify as control deficiency only.
6. Identify which critical IT functionality is at risk.
7. Decide whether the objective failure could cause an
   undetected failure of that functionality.
8. Decide whether, considering business controls, a failure
   could lead to a material misstatement — if yes at every
   stage, the deficiency is a [[Material Weakness]].
9. If below material-weakness risk, judge whether it still
   warrants audit-committee disclosure as a [[Significant Deficiency]].
10. Perform a "reasonable person" review and confirm
    aggregation of control risk has been considered.

## SOX relevance

- Provides the standard methodology auditors use to keep ITGC
  findings (e.g., weak change management or [[Access Controls]] over a financial application) proportionate —
  avoiding automatic escalation of every ITGC exception to a
  [[Material Weakness]] under the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]].
- Reinforces that [[Internal Control]] evaluation is
  risk-based: only ITGCs tied through the reliance chain to key
  automated controls and data security matter for [[ICFR]].
- Explicitly considers unauthorized-change risk to data, and
  the role of compensating business-process controls and
  [[Segregation of Duties]] in mitigating ITGC failures.
- Includes a glossary of terms and an appendix excerpting the
  full [[GAIT Methodology]].
