## Overview

[[AS 2201]] (originally Auditing Standard No. 5, adopted by PCAOB
Release 2007-005A) is the [[PCAOB]] standard applied when an auditor is
engaged to audit management's assessment of the effectiveness of
[[ICFR]] integrated with the financial statement audit, as required for
accelerated filers by [[SOX Section 404]](b) of the
[[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]]. The auditor's objective is to express an opinion
on the effectiveness of ICFR; because ICFR cannot be considered
effective if one or more [[Material Weakness]]es exist, the auditor must
obtain sufficient evidence for reasonable assurance about whether
material weaknesses exist as of the date of management's assessment.
The auditor must use the same suitable, recognized control framework as
management (in practice, the [[COSO Internal Control Framework]]).

## Audit approach

### Integration and planning
- The [[Integrated Audit]] designs control tests to serve both opinions:
  the ICFR opinion as of year-end and control-risk assessments for the
  financial statement audit.
- Planning considers industry and company matters, prior knowledge,
  materiality (same as the financial statement audit), previously
  communicated deficiencies, and complexity — with explicit direction on
  **scaling** for smaller, less complex companies.
- **Risk assessment underlies the entire audit**: more attention where
  material-weakness risk is highest; no need to test controls that,
  even if deficient, present no reasonable possibility of material
  misstatement. [[Fraud Risk]] and [[Management Override]] controls
  (significant unusual transactions, journal entries, related parties,
  estimates, incentive-mitigating controls) must be addressed.

### Top-down approach
- The auditor uses a [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]]: begin at the
  financial statement level, focus on [[Entity-Level Controls]], then
  work down to significant accounts and disclosures and their relevant
  assertions (existence/occurrence, completeness, valuation, rights and
  obligations, presentation and disclosure).
- Entity-level controls vary in precision (indirect control-environment
  controls; monitoring controls that reduce other testing; controls
  precise enough to sufficiently address a risk on their own). The
  auditor **must** evaluate the control environment and the
  [[Period-End Financial Reporting Process]].
- Understanding likely sources of misstatement ("what could go wrong")
  includes understanding transaction flows and IT's effect on them —
  [[IT General Controls]] and [[IT Application Controls]] are assessed
  within the top-down approach, not as a separate evaluation.
  [[Walkthrough]]s (inquiry, observation, inspection, re-performance)
  are usually the most effective way to achieve these objectives and
  must be performed by the auditor or under direct supervision.

### Testing controls
- Test **design effectiveness** (walkthroughs ordinarily suffice) and
  **operating effectiveness** (control operates as designed; performer
  has authority and competence). A smaller company with limited
  [[Segregation of Duties]] may achieve control objectives through
  alternative controls, which the auditor evaluates.
- Evidence needed scales with the risk associated with the control;
  automated controls are generally lower risk when relevant ITGCs are
  effective. Test nature (inquiry < observation < inspection <
  re-performance; inquiry alone is never sufficient), timing
  (roll-forward from interim testing), and extent vary with risk.
- Subsequent-year audits may use prior knowledge, benchmarking of
  automated controls, and must vary testing to introduce
  unpredictability.

## Using the work of others

- The auditor should evaluate the extent to which to use work performed
  by internal auditors, other company personnel, and third parties
  working under management or [[Audit Committee]] direction, based on
  their **competence and objectivity** (per AS 2605); as control risk
  increases, the auditor must do more of the work personally.

## Evaluating deficiencies

- Evaluate the severity of each deficiency (individually and in
  combination): reasonable possibility of failure to prevent/detect a
  misstatement, and magnitude of the potential misstatement —
  considering [[Compensating Controls]] and the "prudent official" test.
- Indicators of a [[Material Weakness]]: senior-management fraud;
  restatement to correct a material misstatement; an auditor-identified
  material misstatement that ICFR would not have detected; ineffective
  [[Audit Committee]] oversight.
- All material weaknesses must be communicated in writing to management
  and the audit committee before the report is issued;
  [[Significant Deficiency]] items to the audit committee; all other
  deficiencies to management.

## Opinions and reporting

- The auditor forms an opinion from all evidence (control tests,
  financial-statement misstatements, internal audit reports) and issues
  a report with required elements: opinion on whether the company
  maintained, in all material respects, effective ICFR based on the
  control criteria; basis for opinion; definition and inherent
  limitations of ICFR.
- One material weakness compels an **adverse opinion**; a scope
  limitation compels a disclaimer or withdrawal. Written management
  representations are required (refusal is a scope limitation).
- The auditor also evaluates whether management's required Item 308
  report elements are complete and properly presented.
