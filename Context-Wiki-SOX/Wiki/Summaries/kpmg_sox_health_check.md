## Overview

This [[KPMG]] brief (© 2016) describes the **SOX Health
Check**, an assessment of the health and maturity of a
company's Internal Controls Over Financial Reporting
([[ICFR]], which KPMG abbreviates "ICOFR") program. KPMG
uses a medical analogy: the Health Check is "like a
physical at the doctor's office" — it diagnoses pain points
and develops a prescription for better health, and
companies should have regular check-ups rather than wait
for symptoms (it is more expensive and stressful to
remediate after something goes wrong or after a "close
call"). KPMG observes that [[ICFR]] programs often focus on
compliance with the [[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]], with less focus
on identifying control efficiencies or value-add
activities.

## Why ICFR health matters

A healthy, strategically aligned [[ICFR]] program helps to:

- Ensure a strong 404a process (management's assessment
  under [[SOX Section 404]]).
- Reduce the impact of control issues.
- Avoid negative economic impacts related to a
  [[Material Weakness]].
- Develop stronger controls that enhance company
  performance.
- Avoid erosion of [[Audit Committee]] confidence.
- Focus on the [[Total Cost of Control]].
- Drive improvements through a culture that embraces
  change.

## What the SOX Health Check assesses

Using quantitative and qualitative criteria, it benchmarks
the company on questions such as:

- What is the strategy for the ICFR program?
- How is the [[Total Cost of Control]] managed?
- Does the [[SOX Compliance Program]] add value to the
  company and its processes?
- How effective is the ICFR risk assessment
  ([[Top-Down Risk Assessment]])?
- How is technology leveraged, and what level of
  automation exists in the control portfolio?
- How does the company align with its
  [[External Auditor]]s?

Actionable outcomes: lower total cost of control, improved
ICFR governance, enhanced team performance, and improved
program efficiency.

## KPMG's approach

1. **Understand key areas of focus** — current program and
   concerns, strategies, prior control testing results,
   benchmark comparisons.
2. **Assess current-state maturity** — review
   documentation against KPMG's maturity continuum;
   interview stakeholders on current and desired future
   state. KPMG uses a proprietary maturity model measuring
   almost **150 qualitative and quantitative data points**.
3. **Establish a future-state roadmap** — gap analysis
   between current and desired maturity, improvement
   areas, project profiles, and a project calendar.

## The seven pillars and the maturity continuum

KPMG's Maturity Continuum rates the program across the
pillars of a Sarbanes-Oxley program, each with lower- and
higher-maturity characteristics:

| Pillar | Lower maturity | Higher maturity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strategy | Basic compliance driven | Value-driven culture |
| Risk Assessment | Aged or unclear scoping | Identifies emerging issues |
| [[Entity-Level Controls]] (ELCs) | Undeveloped enterprise view | Integrates with enterprise |
| Control Selection | Controls not aligned to business | Risk and control advisor |
| Testing Strategy | Unclear or misaligned | Efficient and evolving |
| Evaluating Results | Exception scorekeeper | Proactive management of root causes |
| Governance | Fragmented accountability | Innovative and aligned |

## Controls Portfolio Analysis (CPAM)

Performed alone, the Health Check is an objective [[ICFR]]
program review. Combined with KPMG's **Controls Portfolio
Analysis (CPAM)**, it adds a comprehensive quantitative
view of the controls portfolio, including the
[[Total Cost of Control]] and its components. CPAM supports
[[Controls Rationalization]] efforts and process
improvements — reducing risks and costs, increasing
reliance, and driving value.
