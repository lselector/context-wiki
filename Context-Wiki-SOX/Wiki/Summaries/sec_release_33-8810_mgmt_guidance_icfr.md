## Overview

SEC Release Nos. 33-8810 / 34-55929 (effective June 27, 2007) is the
[[SEC]]'s interpretive guidance for management on evaluating and assessing
[[ICFR]] as required by the rules implementing [[SOX Section 404]] of the
[[Sarbanes-Oxley Act]] (Exchange Act Rules 13a-15(c) and 15d-15(c)).
An evaluation that complies with this guidance is one way — voluntary, not
exclusive — to satisfy the annual evaluation requirement. Companion Release
33-8809 amended the rules to say so and codified the definition of
[[Material Weakness]]. The guidance was deliberately aligned with the
[[PCAOB]]'s [[AS 2201]] (Auditing Standard No. 5), while preserving the
different roles of management and the [[External Auditor]].

## Two organizing principles

1. **Adequacy of controls:** Management should evaluate whether it has
   implemented controls that adequately address the risk that a material
   misstatement of the financial statements would not be prevented or
   detected in a timely manner — a [[Top-Down Risk Assessment]] in which
   [[Entity-Level Controls]] play a central role.
2. **Risk-based evidence:** Management's evaluation of evidence about the
   operation of its controls should be based on its assessment of risk —
   more efficient methods (e.g., self-assessment, [[Management Assessment Process|Daily Interaction]],
   ongoing monitoring) in low-risk areas, more extensive direct testing in
   high-risk areas.

The guidance is principles-based, provides no checklist, and is explicitly
scalable so smaller public companies can tailor procedures to their size
and complexity. Management must use a suitable control framework (the
[[COSO Internal Control Framework]] is cited as an example, along with
CoCo and the Turnbull Report).

## The evaluation process

### Identifying financial reporting risks and controls
- Start from how GAAP applies to the business; identify [[Financial Reporting Risks]] — misstatement risks that could be material,
  including [[Fraud Risk]] and the risk of [[Management Override]].
- Identify preventive and/or detective controls placed in operation that
  adequately address those risks; it is not necessary to identify every
  control or redundant controls.
- **[[Entity-Level Controls]]:** consider their nature and precision.
  Indirect controls (control environment) influence other controls; some
  monitoring controls reduce other testing; controls precise enough to
  prevent or detect material misstatement can eliminate the need to
  evaluate additional controls for that risk.
- **[[IT General Controls]]:** automated and IT-dependent controls rely on
  ITGCs (program development, program change, computer operations, access
  to programs and data). Only ITGCs necessary for the proper and consistent
  operation of other identified controls need evaluation; IT risk
  identification is integral to the top-down approach, not a separate
  evaluation. Related [[IT Application Controls]] (e.g., automated matching,
  edit checks) may be more efficient to evaluate than manual controls.

### Evaluating evidence of operating effectiveness
- Align nature, timing, and extent of evidence with **ICFR risk** — the
  combined risk of the financial reporting element and the control
  (complexity, judgment, risk of failure).
- Evidence may come from direct testing, ongoing monitoring, and — in
  smaller, centralized companies — management's daily interaction with
  controls.
- Operating effectiveness considers whether the control operated as
  designed and whether the person performing it has the necessary
  authority and competence.

### Evidential matter and documentation
- Management must maintain reasonable support (evidential matter) for its
  assessment; form and extent are flexible (memos, flowcharts, policy
  manuals, e-mails) and focused only on the controls that address the
  identified risks.
- Multiple-location considerations: evaluate controls where they operate;
  centralized controls permit a single-location approach; low-risk
  locations may rely on self-assessment plus central monitoring.

## Reporting considerations

- **Deficiency evaluation:** management evaluates the severity of each
  control deficiency — likelihood ("reasonable possibility") and magnitude
  of potential misstatement — individually and in combination, considering
  [[Compensating Controls]] and qualitative risk factors. Indicators of a
  [[Material Weakness]] include senior-management fraud, restatement of
  prior financials, a material misstatement not detected by ICFR, and
  ineffective [[Audit Committee]] oversight. The "prudent official" test
  is the final backstop.
- **Assessment expression:** management must state whether ICFR is
  effective, without qualification; ICFR cannot be assessed effective if
  any material weakness exists. [[Significant Deficiency]] items are
  reported to the audit committee and auditor (per [[SOX Section 302]]
  certification processes) but not disclosed in the annual report.
- **Material weakness disclosure:** describe its nature, impact on
  financial reporting and ICFR, and remediation plans, distinguishing
  pervasive from limited weaknesses.
- **Restatements** do not automatically require reassessing the prior
  ICFR conclusion, but disclosures may need supplementing.
- **Scope limitations are not permitted:** if management cannot assess
  part of ICFR (e.g., a service organization refuses a Type 2 SAS 70
  report), it must decide whether that inability precludes concluding
  ICFR is effective.
